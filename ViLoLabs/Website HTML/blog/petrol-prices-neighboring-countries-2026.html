    Why even compare with neighbours?

    A truck driver I read about was once quoted saying he'd rather top up the tank on one side of the Wagah border than the other — obviously on the Pakistani side. That was in 2019. Back then Pakistan's petrol in INR equivalent was around ₹35. Today it's ₹51. Still nearly half of what you'd pay in Delhi.

    Most petrol price articles in India compare city to city — Delhi vs. Mumbai vs. Chennai. Which is fine. But nobody talks about the bigger picture: where does India actually sit in the neighbourhood? Are we expensive? Are we cheap? And more importantly — why do prices differ so much when we're all importing from the same global crude oil market?

    That's what this post is about. We pulled petrol prices from all 7 of India's neighbours, converted everything to INR at current exchange rates, and put it all in one place. Let's get into it.

    How we converted prices: All local currency prices are converted using mid-market exchange rates as of 24 May 2026 (source: Wise/XE). Petrol prices are national averages or capital city pump prices — they vary within each country just like India's do.

    The data: all 7 countries at a glance

    Here's the full picture before we go country by country. The numbers might surprise you — especially where India ends up in the ranking.

    Pakistan — how is petrol cheaper than water bottles?

    At ₹51.60 per litre (INR equivalent), Pakistan has the cheapest petrol in the neighbourhood. And that number makes people's heads spin — India's cheapest city, Delhi, charges nearly double that.

    The simple explanation: Pakistan's government has been subsidising fuel for decades. It's political, not economic. Removing fuel subsidies causes immediate, visible pain — longer lines, angrier voters. So governments kept prices artificially low by absorbing the difference from the state budget.

    But here's the thing — this game is getting harder to play. Pakistan has been borrowing dollars to import oil, then selling that oil in rapidly-devaluing Pakistani Rupees. The IMF finally stepped in and said: fix this, or no more bailout money. As a result, Pakistani petrol prices have already been hiked 4 times in the last 18 months. The subsidy era is on its way out, just slowly.

    For now though — ₹51.60 is the reality across the border. A 40-litre fill-up costs an equivalent of ₹2,064 in Islamabad. The same fill in Delhi costs ₹3,791.

    Nepal and Bangladesh — cheaper than India, but not for the reason you'd think

    This one genuinely confuses people. Nepal and Bangladesh are landlocked or import-dependent countries that get their petroleum through Indian ports and pipelines. Their fuel literally passes through India. So shouldn't it be more expensive by the time it gets to Kathmandu or Dhaka?

    You'd think so. But Nepal's Nepal Oil Corporation (NOC) pegs prices to Indian rates and often undercuts them — they keep taxes lower to stay affordable for a lower-income population. Nepal at ₹68.75 per litre is cheaper than any Indian city. Even cheaper than Delhi.

    Bangladesh works similarly. The government controls fuel pricing and kept it stable to contain inflation. At 89 BDT per litre — or ₹69.60 in INR equivalent — it's also cheaper than Delhi.

    The lesson here isn't that Nepal and Bangladesh are more efficient. It's that India taxes its fuel very heavily. Central excise duty alone is ₹19+ per litre. Add state VAT and you're often paying 35–55% of the final price as tax. Our neighbours tax less — so despite the same import cost, they charge less at the pump.

    Bhutan — the special case where INR conversion is pointless

    Bhutan is a unique one. The Bhutanese Ngultrum (BTN) is pegged 1:1 to the Indian Rupee — always has been, always will be. They're accepted interchangeably in most of Bhutan. So there's no real "conversion" happening here.

    Bhutan's petrol at 109.85 BTN/litre is essentially the same as saying ₹109.85. That's more expensive than Delhi (₹94.77) but roughly in line with high-tax Indian states like Rajasthan or Maharashtra.

    The reason it costs more than Delhi: Bhutan imports all its petroleum from India, and the transport cost from Indian refineries to Thimphu gets added on. It's not taxes — it's geography. Thimphu is a mountain city. Getting fuel there costs more.

    Sri Lanka — the most expensive, and there's a hard story behind it

    Sri Lanka's petrol at ₹119.04 per litre (INR equivalent) is the most expensive in the neighbourhood, and it didn't happen by accident.

    In 2022, Sri Lanka ran out of foreign exchange almost completely. There was no money to pay for oil imports. Petrol queues stretched for kilometres. Vehicles sat idle for days. Hospitals ran generators on donated fuel. It was genuinely dire.

    The government's response, once the crisis peaked, was to remove all fuel subsidies and let prices float at market rates. This was the condition to attract emergency imports and get back to some level of stability. It worked — pumps are full now — but Sri Lankan consumers are still paying the price. Literally.

    At ₹119/litre, a 40-litre fill-up costs INR equivalent of ₹4,762 in Colombo. Compare that to ₹2,064 in Islamabad. Same region, entirely different economic reality.

    China and Myanmar — the ones nobody talks about

    China at ₹98.54/litre is surprisingly close to India. They follow a market-linked pricing model — the National Development and Reform Commission adjusts prices every 10 days based on international crude prices. No massive subsidies, no aggressive taxing. The result is a price very similar to what Delhi pays.

    One thing worth knowing: petrol in China is very consistent across provinces. There's no "Delhi vs. Andhra Pradesh" situation with a ₹20 range. Central pricing keeps variation tight — usually within ₹3-4 across the country.

    Myanmar is harder to pin down. The official pump price works out to about ₹93.60/litre in INR equivalent. But Myanmar's been through significant political turmoil since 2021, and fuel availability has been inconsistent in many regions. The ₹93.60 figure is the official rate — actual street prices in some areas can be quite different.

    So where does India actually stand?

    India sits 5th out of 8 in this comparison — more expensive than Pakistan, Nepal, Bangladesh and Myanmar, but cheaper than China, Bhutan, and Sri Lanka.

    More importantly, India's petrol tax structure is one of the heaviest in the region. Central and state taxes together make up 40–55% of the final pump price depending on which state you're in. This is how the government generates revenue — fuel taxes are reliable and hard to avoid.

    The counterargument is that countries like Pakistan are just deferring the pain — cheap fuel today via subsidies means higher debt and eventual price hikes. India went through painful deregulation between 2010 and 2014. The transition wasn't fun, but at least we're not managing an artificial price that distorts the whole economy.

    That said — ₹94.77 in Delhi while your neighbour in Kathmandu pays ₹68.75 for the same litre? That's a gap worth understanding. And now you do.

    Questions people usually ask

    Why can't India just reduce petrol taxes like Nepal does?
    It's not that simple unfortunately. India's state and central governments collect roughly ₹4–5 lakh crore per year from fuel taxes. That money funds roads, subsidies, defence, salaries. A meaningful tax cut would require finding that revenue somewhere else — income tax, GST, borrowing. Every time there's a global oil price dip, there's pressure to cut fuel taxes. And every time, the government prefers to keep the revenue. It's not a conspiracy — it's a budget reality.
    Is it legal to bring petrol from Nepal or Bangladesh into India?
    No. Bringing petrol across international borders — even personal quantities — falls under customs regulations and is generally not permitted. Border residents have tried this for obvious reasons but it's illegal. Don't try it.
    Pakistan's petrol is cheap — does that mean their economy is doing well?
    Actually the opposite. Cheap petrol via government subsidies is often a sign of economic stress, not strength. Pakistan has been in an IMF bailout programme, their rupee has depreciated significantly, and they've raised petrol prices multiple times under international pressure. The cheap pump price is a short-term political choice with long-term fiscal consequences.
    Will India's petrol prices come down anytime soon?
    Hard to say. Prices were last revised in May 2026 (₹3/litre hike due to West Asia supply tensions). Any reduction would require either a sharp fall in global crude, or a political decision to cut excise duty. Election years sometimes see cuts — the 2021 and 2023 cuts both happened close to state elections. Outside of that, don't hold your breath.
